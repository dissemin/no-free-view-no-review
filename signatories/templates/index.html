The knowledge developed by academic research is the common heritage of mankind. Sadly, this heritage is only available to a chosen few, as it is published in subscription-only journals and locked away behind paywalls. Scientific publishers benefit from this model by charging expensive subscription fees to universities and libraries, even though this money does not contribute to the research itself. It is now obvious that this outdated and unethical model should be changed, with research articles made available for free on the Internet for everyone. The modest cost of online publications, which are far less than the current subscription costs, should not be covered by readers, but by universities, research agencies, and other actors.

The current model is made possible by the volunteer work of researchers, in two ways: publishing their research in closed-access venues, and serving as unpaid peer reviewers to evaluate the submitted research. We understand that publication practices are slow to change, because the prestige attached to existing journals is an important part of how researchers is evaluated, and because publication is often a collaborative effort involving students and other untenured researchers. However, the choice to review is an individual one, often performed by more senior researchers. Thus, we hope that by allocating our reviewing time in priority to open-access venues, we can contribute to their quality and shift the publication model away from closed-access venues.

For this reason, we pledge to give priority to open-access venues in how we allocate our reviewing time, program committee or board membership, and organizational efforts. We pledge that we will avoid serving as reviewers for venues which will not make publicly available the research that we have reviewed. We reserve the right to make exceptions to this policy when warranted by the scientific interest, or if we wish to reciprocate by giving a fair number of reviews to venues where we have published
